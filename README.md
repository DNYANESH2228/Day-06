Phishing Email Analysis – Day 6
Email Sample 1 – Fake American Express Account Suspension
Identified Red Flags:

The sender email domain is not official (pentagon-seguridad.cl instead of americanexpress.com).

The email uses urgency and threat language like “temporary suspension” to force quick action.

Psychological Manipulation Used:

Fear of losing account access.

Authority misuse by using the American Express name and logo to build false trust.

Possible Impact:

User may enter login credentials on a fake website.

This can lead to financial fraud or identity theft.

Prevention Steps:

Always verify the sender’s email domain before clicking any link.

Visit the official website manually instead of using email links.






Email Sample 2 – Fake AT&T Order Confirmation
Identified Red Flags:

The sender domain (wireless-att-mail.com) is not the official AT&T domain.

The attachment link ends in “.pdf.jar,” which indicates a potentially malicious executable file.

Psychological Manipulation Used:

Financial fear by showing an expensive iPhone purchase.

Confusion and urgency to make the user click quickly.

Possible Impact:

Malware may be installed on the device.

Attackers could steal passwords, banking details, or control the system.

Prevention Steps:

Do not download unexpected attachments or click unknown links.

Confirm order details directly through the official company website






Email Sample 3 – Fake Investment / Minister Scam (Google Forms)
🔴 Identified Red Flags:

The email promises a huge amount of money (€47,745,533) in exchange for partnership, which is unrealistic and suspicious.

The sender claims to be a government minister but uses a normal email address (kakao.com), not an official government domain.

🧠 Psychological Manipulation Used:

Greed and reward tactic – offering 30% of a large amount of money to attract the victim.

Authority tactic – claiming to be a minister to appear powerful and trustworthy.

⚠️ Possible Impact:

The attacker may ask for bank details or personal information.


The victim may be asked to pay “processing fees” and lose money in advance-fee fraud.
